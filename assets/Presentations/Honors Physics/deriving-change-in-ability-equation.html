Where We Are

From our investigations, we discovered five patterns:

3 Direction Patterns
2 Amount Patterns

Today's challenge: find one mathematical statement
that agrees with all five.

Quick recap. We have 5 crazy ideas from our observations. Three tell us about direction — when ability increases, decreases, or stays the same. Two tell us about amount — how force strength and displacement distance affect how much ability changes. Today we're going to turn all 5 into one equation.
Direction Pattern 1

Increasing Ability

In order to increase the potential ability or ability stored in a system, an external system has to exert a force in the same direction as the displacement of things in the system.
Start with the first direction pattern. Show the visual — force and displacement point the same way, and ability increases. This is what we saw with the brick being lifted, the launcher being pulled back, and the cart being pushed forward. The arrows always pointed the same way when ability went up.
Direction Pattern 2

Decreasing Ability

In order to decrease the potential ability or ability stored in a system, an external system has to exert a force in the opposite direction as the displacement of things in the system.
Second direction pattern. Now force and displacement point opposite ways — like when you lower a brick (force up from your hand, displacement down) or when friction slows the cart (force backward, displacement forward). Ability goes down.
Direction Pattern 3

No Change in Ability

An external system fails to change the potential ability or ability stored in a system if it cannot displace things in the system or if it exerts a force that is perpendicular to the displacement of things in the system.

In both cases: Δability = 0

Two ways ability doesn't change: 1) the force is perpendicular to the displacement (like the rail pushing up on the cart rolling horizontally — those are at 90 degrees), or 2) there's no displacement at all (you push on a wall but nothing moves). Both give zero change in ability.
Amount Pattern 4

Stronger Force = More Ability

When an external system displaces a system the same amount, the system gains more ability when the force it exerts on the system is stronger.

Small force → some ability gained

Bigger force → MORE ability gained

This is what students observed with the meter stick and cart. Same displacement, but when you push harder, the cart gains more ability. Force and ability gained are directly related.
Amount Pattern 5

More Displacement = More Ability

When an external system exerts a same strength force on a system, the system gains more ability when it displaces the system further.

Short push → some ability gained

Long push → MORE ability gained

Same experiment with meter stick and cart, but now force is constant and we vary displacement. Push the cart further with the same force, and it gains more ability. Displacement and ability gained are directly related too.

What Do We Know?

Let's start simple. What quantities affect the change in ability?

From patterns 4 & 5, the change in ability depends on:

F

Force exerted
by external system

and

Δx

Displacement
of things in the system

$\Delta\text{ability} = f(\vec{F},\ \Delta\vec{x})$

But how do F and Δx combine?

Guide students to recognize that the two quantities that matter are F and delta-x. We write this as "change in ability is some function of F and delta-x". The question is: what's the function? How do they combine? Do we add them? Multiply them? Something else?

How Do F and Δx Combine?

Let's consider the simplest possibilities:

Addition?

$\Delta\text{ability} = F + \Delta x$

Multiplication?

$\Delta\text{ability} = F \cdot \Delta x$

Division?

$\Delta\text{ability} = \dfrac{F}{\Delta x}$

Subtraction?

$\Delta\text{ability} = F - \Delta x$

Let's test each against our 5 patterns.

Write all four candidates on the board. Ask students: which of these did your group come up with? Now we need to test them. A good equation has to agree with ALL 5 crazy ideas, not just some. Let's be scientists and test them systematically.

Test: What If Δx = 0?

Pattern 3 says: if you can't displace things, ability doesn't change.

So Δability must equal 0 when Δx = 0.

$F + 0 = F \neq 0$
✗ Addition fails!
$F \cdot 0 = 0$
✓ Multiplication works!
$\frac{F}{0}$ = undefined
✗ Division breaks!
$F - 0 = F \neq 0$
✗ Subtraction fails!
Plug in delta-x = 0 and see what survives. Addition gives F, which isn't zero — fails. Multiplication gives zero — passes! Division by zero is undefined — fails hard. Subtraction gives F — also fails. Only multiplication survives this test. But we still need to check the remaining patterns.

Only One Candidate Remains

$\Delta\text{ability} = F \cdot \Delta x$

But does it agree with all five patterns?

TASK

With your group, test $F \cdot \Delta x$ against each of the 5 patterns.
Does it agree with every single one?

Give groups 2-3 minutes to verify F times delta-x against all 5 patterns. They should check: same direction gives positive, opposite gives negative, perpendicular gives zero, bigger F gives bigger result, bigger delta-x gives bigger result. Walk around and check their reasoning.

Checking Direction Patterns

How does $F \cdot \Delta x$ handle direction?

Same direction:
$(+) \cdot (+) = $ positive
→ ability increases ✓
Opposite direction:
$(-) \cdot (+) = $ negative
→ ability decreases ✓
Perpendicular:
F has no component along Δx
→ ability unchanged ✓
Walk through each direction pattern. Same direction means both are positive (or both negative), so the product is positive — ability increases. Opposite means one is positive and one negative, product is negative — ability decreases. Perpendicular means the force has no component along the displacement, so the product is zero — no change. All three direction patterns check out!

Checking Amount Patterns

How does $F \cdot \Delta x$ handle amounts?

Same Δx, stronger F:

$\underbrace{F_{\text{big}}}_{\uparrow} \cdot \Delta x = \text{bigger result}$

✓ More ability gained

Same F, further Δx:

$F \cdot \underbrace{\Delta x_{\text{big}}}_{\uparrow} = \text{bigger result}$

✓ More ability gained

Multiplication captures both amount patterns!

Amount check: if you keep delta-x constant and make F bigger, F times delta-x gives a bigger number — more ability. If you keep F constant and make delta-x bigger, same thing — bigger result. Multiplication naturally captures the idea that both quantities contribute proportionally to the change in ability.

All Five Patterns ✓

# Pattern $F \cdot \Delta x$ says... Result
1 Same direction → increase $(+)(+) > 0$ ✓
2 Opposite direction → decrease $(-)(+) < 0$ ✓
3 Perpendicular or no Δx → no change $= 0$ ✓
4 Same Δx, bigger F → more ability $F_{\uparrow} \cdot \Delta x = \text{bigger}$ ✓
5 Same F, bigger Δx → more ability $F \cdot \Delta x_{\uparrow} = \text{bigger}$ ✓
Build the table row by row. Each pattern passes. This is a powerful moment — a single simple equation captures all five observations. Let students see how elegant this is. Pause and celebrate the discovery.

Our Mathematical Statement

$\Delta\text{ability} = \vec{F} \cdot \Delta\vec{x}$

The change in ability done on a system
equals the force exerted by an external system
multiplied by the displacement of things in the system.

This is the big payoff. The simplest mathematical statement that agrees with all five patterns is F dot delta-x. The vector arrows remind us that direction matters — that's what patterns 1-3 taught us. The multiplication captures patterns 4-5 about amounts. One equation, five patterns. This is what physics is about: finding simple math that describes complex observations.

But Wait...

Our patterns covered forces that are
perfectly along, perfectly opposite, or perfectly perpendicular
to the displacement.

What if the force is at an angle?

Pause here for dramatic effect. We tested 0 degrees, 180 degrees, and 90 degrees. But forces in the real world aren't always perfectly aligned. What happens when you push something at a 30-degree angle? Or 60 degrees? Our equation needs to handle that too.

Force at an Angle

Imagine pushing a cart forward, but your force is angled upward.

TASK

The force is neither along the displacement nor perpendicular to it.
Does all of the force change the system's ability? Or only part of it?

Set up the problem visually. The force is at an angle theta to the displacement. Ask: does the entire force contribute to changing ability? Students should connect this to Pattern 3 — the perpendicular part doesn't change ability. So only the part of the force that's along the displacement matters. Give groups 1-2 minutes to discuss.

Breaking the Force into Parts

We can split the force into two components:

Only the component along the displacement changes ability.

This is the key insight. We can decompose any force into a part along the displacement and a part perpendicular to it. Pattern 3 already told us: perpendicular forces don't change ability. So only the parallel component matters. This is a direct consequence of what we already discovered — no new physics needed, just applying what we know.

How Big Is F∥ ?

If we know the angle θ between the force and the displacement...

From the right triangle, the adjacent side is:

$F_{\parallel} = F \cdot \cos(\theta)$
TASK

If only $F_{\parallel}$ changes ability, rewrite our equation using $F \cdot \cos(\theta)$.
Write your updated equation on your whiteboard.

Students already know trig from math class. The parallel component is the adjacent side of the right triangle formed by decomposing F. Adjacent over hypotenuse is cosine. So F-parallel equals F times cos(theta). Now ask them: if only the parallel component matters, what's the updated equation? Give them 1-2 minutes. They should get delta-ability = F cos(theta) times delta-x.

The Complete Equation

$\Delta\text{ability} = F \cdot \Delta x \cdot \cos(\theta)$

where θ is the angle between the force and the displacement

Here it is — the complete equation. The cosine of theta captures the direction relationship. F and delta-x capture the amounts. Everything from our 5 patterns is in this one equation, and now it works for ANY angle, not just the special cases of 0, 90, and 180 degrees.

Does cos(θ) Agree With Our Patterns?

Let's check the three direction patterns using the special angles:

Same direction
$\theta = 0° \quad\rightarrow\quad \cos(0°) = $ +1
Δability = +FΔx ✓
Perpendicular
$\theta = 90° \quad\rightarrow\quad \cos(90°) = $ 0
Δability = 0 ✓
Opposite
$\theta = 180° \quad\rightarrow\quad \cos(180°) = $ -1
Δability = -FΔx ✓

The cos(θ) naturally handles all three direction patterns!

This is the beautiful part. Cos(0) = 1, so same direction gives positive delta-ability — pattern 1 works. Cos(90) = 0, so perpendicular gives zero — pattern 3 works. Cos(180) = -1, so opposite gives negative — pattern 2 works. The cosine function already "knows" about our direction patterns. It's not something extra we bolted on — it's the natural mathematical way to express what our observations told us.

And Everything In Between

The cos(θ) smoothly handles every angle, not just the special cases:

At 45°, about 71% of the force changes ability.
At 60°, only 50% does. The steeper the angle, the less force goes into changing ability.

Show the full spectrum. At 0 degrees, 100% of the force changes ability. As the angle increases, less and less of the force contributes. At 90 degrees, none does. Past 90 degrees, the cosine goes negative — the force is now fighting the displacement and removing ability. At 180 degrees, 100% of the force goes into decreasing ability. The cosine smoothly captures this entire range. This is what makes it the right function.

Give groups 3-4 minutes. Walk around. The 0-degree case is straightforward — 30 units of ability. The 60-degree case: cos(60) = 0.5, so only half the force contributes — 15 units. The 120-degree case: cos(120) = -0.5, negative means ability decreases — the force is partially fighting the displacement. Reveal the answers after discussion. The key insight: same force, same displacement, but the angle completely changes how much ability transfers.

Quick summary of the complete equation's behavior. Every one of our 5 patterns is captured, plus the equation now handles all the in-between angles too. The cosine is the bridge between the qualitative direction patterns and the quantitative equation.

Our Complete Mathematical Statement

$\Delta\text{ability} = F \cdot \Delta x \cdot \cos(\theta)$

Δability — how much the ability of the system changes
F — strength of the force exerted by the external system
Δx — how far the external system displaces things in the system
cos(θ) — the angle between the force and the displacement

The final equation with all its pieces labeled. This is the culmination of everything. Five patterns of observation led to three quantities — force, displacement, and the angle between them — combined into one equation that predicts how much ability any external system transfers to any system. This is physics at its best.
