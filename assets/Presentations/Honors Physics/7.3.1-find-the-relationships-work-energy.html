Section 7.3.1

Mr. Porter  ·  Honors Physics
PIVOTAL CLASS WHITEBOARDS + MARKERS
This is a PIVOTAL class — students derive the expressions for gravitational PE and kinetic energy from the work-energy relationship. Make sure every group has a whiteboard and markers. The goal is to build these expressions from Newton's laws and kinematics — not to just hand them the formulas.

The Scenario

A cable lifts a block from a lower position to a higher position.

The block is already moving upward when we start observing.

Watch the animation — what do you notice about the block's speed?

👀 OBSERVE

Is the block speeding up, slowing down, or moving at constant speed?

Let students watch the animation loop 2-3 times. Ask: "What do you notice about the block's speed as it goes up?" They should observe it speeds up — the spacing between positions increases. Don't label anything yet — students need to translate this into their own sketch in Part A. Ask: "What does the fact that it speeds up tell us about the forces?"
Part A

Sketch the Two States

⬜ WHITEBOARD

Based on what you observed, sketch the initial state and final state of the block.

Include a labeled vertical axis, the block's position, the cable, and velocity arrows at each state.

Label your sketch with these symbols:

Positions: $y_i$ and $y_f$

Speeds: $v_{yi}$ and $v_{yf}$

Which velocity arrow should be longer? Why?

Give groups 2-3 minutes. This is the critical translation step — they go from watching a process to representing two snapshot states. Walk around and check: (1) vertical y-axis with + up, (2) block shown at two heights with y_i below y_f, (3) velocity arrows both pointing up but v_yf arrow longer than v_yi, (4) cable attached. If groups are struggling, ask "What was the block doing at the beginning of what you observed? What was it doing at the end?" This is their reference diagram for everything that follows.
Part B

Work Done by the Cable

⬜ WHITEBOARD

Write an expression for the work the cable does on the block during displacement $y_f - y_i$

Recall: work = force × displacement (when parallel)

$\color{#f5a623}{W_{\text{cable}}} = \color{#5b9cf6}{F_{\text{cable}}} \cdot (y_f - y_i)$

Cable force is upward, displacement is upward → positive work

Key point: the cable force and displacement are in the same direction, so the work is positive. The force is constant (we assume constant acceleration). We'll figure out what F_cable actually equals in the next part.
Part C

Force Diagram → Find $F_{\text{cable}}$

⬜ WHITEBOARD

Draw a force diagram for the block.

Use it to find $F_{\text{cable}}$ in terms of $m$, $a$, and $g$.

Then substitute into the expression from Part B.

Hints:

How many forces act on the block? What are they?

The block accelerates upward — what does that tell you about the relative size of the forces?

Give groups 3-4 minutes. They need to: (1) draw a force diagram with the block as a dot, (2) identify two forces — cable up, gravity down, (3) apply Newton's 2nd law, (4) solve for F_cable, (5) substitute into W = F·Δy from Part B. Walk around and check diagrams before advancing. Common error: forgetting that F_cable > mg because the block accelerates upward.
Part C — Let's Check

Force Diagram & Newton's 2nd Law

Newton's 2nd Law (+ up):

$\color{#5b9cf6}{F_{\text{cable}}} - \color{#e8504a}{mg} = ma$

Solve for $F_{\text{cable}}$:

$\color{#5b9cf6}{F_{\text{cable}}} = ma + mg$

Substitute into Part B:

$\color{#f5a623}{W} = (ma + mg)(y_f - y_i)$
Reveal the diagram first — compare to what groups drew. Key check: did they represent the block as a point? Are there exactly two forces? Is F_cable longer than mg? Then walk through the algebra. Make sure they distribute for the final substitution: W = ma(yf - yi) + mg(yf - yi).
Part D

Replace Acceleration with Speeds

⬜ WHITEBOARD

Use a kinematics equation to convert $a$ into an expression with $v_i$, $v_f$, and $y_f - y_i$

Which kinematics equation connects $v_i$, $v_f$, $a$, and $\Delta y$ without time?

$v_{yf}^2 = v_{yi}^2 + 2a(y_f - y_i)$

Solve for $a$:

$a = \dfrac{v_{yf}^2 - v_{yi}^2}{2(y_f - y_i)}$
Students should recognize that v² = v₀² + 2aΔx is the right kinematics equation because it has no time variable. We need to eliminate a because we want the final result in terms of measurable quantities (positions and speeds), not acceleration. Solving for a is straightforward algebra.
Part E

New Expression for $F_{\text{cable}}$

⬜ WHITEBOARD

Substitute the expression for $a$ from Part D into $F_{\text{cable}}$ from Part C

We had:   $F_{\text{cable}} = ma + mg$

Substituting $a = \dfrac{v_{yf}^2 - v_{yi}^2}{2(y_f - y_i)}$:

$F_{\text{cable}} = m \cdot \dfrac{v_{yf}^2 - v_{yi}^2}{2(y_f - y_i)} + mg$

Force now in terms of $m$, $g$, positions, and speeds — no acceleration!

This is just direct substitution — the algebra isn't hard, but make sure groups are keeping their work organized on the whiteboard. The key insight is that we've eliminated acceleration entirely. Everything is now in terms of quantities we can measure directly.
Part F

Final Work Expression

⬜ WHITEBOARD

Substitute $F_{\text{cable}}$ from Part E into $W_{\text{cable}}$ from Part B

$W_{\text{cable}} = F_{\text{cable}} \cdot (y_f - y_i)$

$W_{\text{cable}} = \left[\, m \cdot \dfrac{v_{yf}^2 - v_{yi}^2}{2(y_f - y_i)} + mg \,\right] \cdot (y_f - y_i)$

Distribute $(y_f - y_i)$ through each term:

$W_{\text{cable}} = \dfrac{m(v_{yf}^2 - v_{yi}^2)}{2} + mg(y_f - y_i)$

Notice: $(y_f - y_i)$ cancels in the first term!

The key algebra moment: when you multiply the fraction by (yf - yi), the denominator cancels! This is satisfying and important. The displacement cancellation in the kinetic energy term is what makes KE depend only on speed, not position. Give groups time to work through the distribution carefully.
Part G

Separate the Terms

Expand and rearrange to see the structure

$\color{#f5a623}{W_{\text{cable}}} = \color{#22d47a}{mg \, y_f} - \color{#22d47a}{mg \, y_i} + \color{#ff6b9d}{\tfrac{1}{2}mv_{yf}^2} - \color{#ff6b9d}{\tfrac{1}{2}mv_{yi}^2}$

The work splits into a sum of changes of two quantities:

Change in Position

$\color{#22d47a}{mg \, y_f - mg \, y_i}$

Depends on where the block is

Change in Speed

$\color{#ff6b9d}{\tfrac{1}{2}mv_{yf}^2 - \tfrac{1}{2}mv_{yi}^2}$

Depends on how fast the block moves

This is the big moment. The work done by the cable naturally splits into two kinds of changes. One depends on position (height) and the other depends on speed. Ask students: "Does it make sense that lifting something higher AND making it go faster both require the cable to do work?" Yes — the cable has to fight gravity AND accelerate the block.

Naming These Quantities

We give these expressions names because they appear everywhere in physics

GRAVITATIONAL POTENTIAL ENERGY

$\color{#22d47a}{U_g = mgy}$

KINETIC ENERGY

$\color{#ff6b9d}{K = \tfrac{1}{2}mv^2}$

So the work equation becomes:

$\color{#f5a623}{W_{\text{cable}}} = \color{#22d47a}{\Delta U_g} + \color{#ff6b9d}{\Delta K}$
These names capture what the expressions physically represent. Gravitational PE depends on position relative to Earth — it's energy stored in the block-Earth system due to their separation. Kinetic energy depends on speed — it's the energy of motion. The work-energy equation says the cable's work goes into changing BOTH of these. This is the work-energy theorem in its most useful form for this chapter.

Walk through it left to right: we started with a definition of work, used Newton's 2nd law to find the force, used kinematics to eliminate acceleration, substituted everything together, and what fell out naturally were expressions for gravitational PE and kinetic energy. What This Means Physically

GRAVITATIONAL PE

$U_g = mgy$

Energy stored in the block–Earth system due to separation

Increases when block goes higher

KINETIC ENERGY

$K = \tfrac{1}{2}mv^2$

Energy of motion — depends on speed, not direction

Always positive (or zero)

Emphasize the physical meaning. The cable does work that goes into TWO things: (1) lifting the block higher increases gravitational PE, and (2) speeding it up increases kinetic energy. The bar chart makes this visual — both bars grow from y_i to y_f, and the total growth equals the work done by the cable. This is the foundation for conservation of energy discussions.

Discussion

💭 GROUP DISCUSSION

Why does gravitational PE depend on position but kinetic energy depends on speed?

💭 THINK DEEPER

What would change if the cable lowered the block while it was speeding up?

Hint: Think about the signs of each term in the work equation

First question: PE comes from the mg·(yf-yi) term — it's about the force of gravity and how far you move against it. KE comes from the kinematics — it's about how fast you're going, which relates to how much force was applied over what distance. Second question: if the block goes down, delta_y is negative so delta_Ug is negative (PE decreases). If it speeds up, delta_K is positive. The cable could actually do LESS work (or negative work) if gravity is doing the accelerating. This previews energy conservation.

Key Takeaways

We derived $U_g$ and $K$ from Newton's Laws + Kinematics — they weren't given to us

Gravitational PE $= mgy$ — energy of position in a gravitational field

Kinetic energy $= \tfrac{1}{2}mv^2$ — energy of motion

Work by external forces = $\color{#22d47a}{\Delta U_g} + \color{#ff6b9d}{\Delta K}$

→ Conservation of Energy

Wrap up by emphasizing: we didn't just memorize formulas — we BUILT them from fundamental physics. This derivation is the foundation for everything else in the energy unit.
