Do Now

Practicing Our
Change in Ability
Equation

$\Delta\text{ability} = F \cdot \Delta x \cdot \cos(\theta)$

Honors Physics — Mr. Porter

Do Now opener. Students should have their whiteboards ready. The equation is displayed as a reference — they derived it last class, so this is pure practice. Give them about 30 seconds to get settled and read the slide before advancing.

Problem 1: Which Way Does Ability Change?

For each scenario, determine: does ability increase, decrease, or stay the same?

Scenario A

You push a cart forward.
It moves forward.

Scenario B

A sliding box feels friction.
The box moves right.

Scenario C

The table pushes up on
a cart rolling sideways.

WHITEBOARD

For each scenario, write A: ___ B: ___ C: ___ using +, −, or 0.

A:
+ Increase

same direction → cos(0°) = +1

B:
− Decrease

opposite → cos(180°) = −1

C:
0 No Change

perpendicular → cos(90°) = 0

Warm-up problem — purely qualitative. Students should recognize the three direction patterns from last class. Give 1-2 minutes for whiteboards. When revealing answers, connect each to the cosine: A is 0 degrees (cos = +1, positive), B is 180 degrees (cos = -1, negative), C is 90 degrees (cos = 0, zero change). Quick consensus check — most students should get this right.

Problem 2: How Much Ability Changes

You lift a brick straight up with a 25 N force. The brick moves up 1.8 m.

What is Δability?

WHITEBOARD

Show your work using the equation. Include the angle and units.

$\Delta\text{ability} = (25\text{ N})(1.8\text{ m})\cos(0°) = (25)(1.8)(1) = $ 45 N·m

Ability increases by 45 N·m — your hand stores ability in the system.

First quantitative problem. Force and displacement are in the same direction, so theta = 0 and cos(0) = 1. This is the simplest possible calculation — students just need to plug in and multiply. The key teaching moment is the UNITS: Newtons times meters gives N·m. Don't name this unit "Joules" yet! Just let them see that the unit of change in ability is N·m. Give 2-3 minutes for whiteboards. Common mistake: forgetting to state the angle or the cosine term. Reinforce that even when it equals 1, we should show it.

Problem 3: Friction Removes Ability

A crate slides across a rough floor. Friction exerts a 12 N force backward. The crate slides 3.0 m forward.

What is Δability from friction?

WHITEBOARD

What angle goes with "opposite"? Show your work.

$\Delta\text{ability} = (12\text{ N})(3.0\text{ m})\cos(180°) = (12)(3.0)(-1) = $ −36 N·m

Ability decreases by 36 N·m — friction removes ability from the system.

This problem introduces the negative sign. Force and displacement are opposite, so theta = 180 degrees and cos(180) = -1. The negative result means friction removes ability from the system. Key misconception to watch for: students who put theta = 0 because they "already accounted for direction" by making F negative. Reinforce: F is always a positive magnitude in this equation — the cosine handles the direction. Give 2-3 minutes.

Problem 4: Force at an Angle

You pull a cart with a 40 N force at 37° above horizontal. The cart moves 5.0 m forward.

Hint: cos(37°) = 0.80

What is Δability?

WHITEBOARD

Show each step. Why isn't it just 40 × 5?

$\Delta\text{ability} = (40\text{ N})(5.0\text{ m})\cos(37°) = (40)(5.0)(0.80) = $ 160 N·m

Only 80% of the force acts along the displacement — the rest pushes up (perpendicular) and doesn't change ability.

This is the hardest problem — it requires the full equation with a non-trivial angle. Give the cosine value so they don't get stuck on calculator mechanics. The key insight to emphasize: 40 N times 5 m would give 200, but only 80% of the force is along the displacement. The other 20% pushes up and doesn't change the cart's ability. Ask: "Why isn't it 200?" and let them articulate that the angle matters. Give 3-4 minutes for this one. If students finish early, ask them: what would happen if you pulled at 60 degrees instead? (Less ability would change because cos(60) = 0.5.)

What We Practiced

✓

Direction — the sign of Δability tells us if ability is gained or lost

Same direction = positive, opposite = negative, perpendicular = zero

✓

Amount — bigger F or bigger Δx means more ability changes

The units of Δability are N·m (Newtons times meters)

✓

Angles — only the part of the force along Δx changes ability

cos(θ) handles this automatically

$\Delta\text{ability} = F \cdot \Delta x \cdot \cos(\theta)$
Quick wrap-up. Reinforce the three skills they just practiced: reading the sign, computing the amount, and handling angles. The equation shown at the bottom is their reference for today's work. Transition into whatever comes next — this Do Now should take about 8-10 minutes total.
