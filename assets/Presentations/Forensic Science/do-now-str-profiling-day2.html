The Scenario

Arson at the Eastside Warehouse

🔥  Last week, a fire destroyed an abandoned warehouse on the east side of town. Arson investigators determined the fire was intentionally set — and found a cigarette butt at the origin point of the blaze.
🚬  DNA was extracted from saliva on the cigarette. However, heat and smoke contamination degraded the sample — the lab was only able to recover reliable results at 4 of 6 STR loci. The remaining two loci are inconclusive.
🚨  Four suspects were identified. Each provided a DNA sample. Your job: Use the partial STR profile to eliminate who you can — and explain why the remaining suspects can't be ruled out yet.
Read the scenario out loud or let students read independently. Key framing: this is realistic — degraded samples are extremely common in arson cases because heat destroys DNA. The FBI's CODIS system uses 20 CODIS core STR loci specifically because more loci = more discriminating power even when some fail. Remind students: even a partial profile is useful. It can eliminate people. What it can't always do is definitively identify one person.
Quick Refresher

What is an STR?

🧬 Short Tandem Repeat A short DNA sequence that repeats over and over at a specific location on a chromosome
🔢 Allele Numbers The number tells you how many times the sequence repeats — e.g., 14, 17 means 14 repeats on one chromosome and 17 on the other
📍 Multiple Loci Forensic labs check STRs at several different locations (loci) in the genome — the combination is unique to each person
🔥 Degraded Samples Heat, UV, or bacteria can break down DNA strands — some loci may fail to produce results, leaving gaps in the profile

A mismatch at any available locus = eliminated. But gaps mean some suspects may remain unresolved.

Quick review — spend 30 seconds here max. New concept today: the 4th pill. Students need to understand that "no result" at a locus is NOT a match AND is NOT a mismatch — it's simply missing data. Analogy: Imagine trying to identify someone from a partial fingerprint. You can still eliminate people, but you may not be able to make a positive ID. Key distinction: DEGRADED ≠ inconclusive for everyone. You can still eliminate suspects who mismatch at the loci that DID produce results.

ANSWER IN YOUR NOTEBOOK
Q1. Which suspect(s) can be confidently eliminated? Cite the specific locus (or loci) that rules them out.
Q2. Which suspect(s) cannot be eliminated based on the available data? How many loci matched, and which ones?
Q3. Why can't investigators definitively name one suspect from this data? What would they need to resolve the ambiguity?
Q4. A defense attorney argues the degraded sample makes all DNA evidence unreliable. How would you respond as the forensic scientist?

Use this slide for class discussion. Reveal the table first, then the four answer boxes. Discussion prompts: — "Both B and C match at 4 loci. Does that mean they're equally likely to be the source?" (Not necessarily — one might be ruled out by other evidence, alibi, etc.) — "Suspect D had a mismatch at only ONE allele at TH01. Is one allele enough to eliminate?" (YES — both alleles must match. One mismatch = eliminated.) — "What could investigators do if they really needed to resolve B vs C?" (Re-amplify the DNA with different primers targeting FGA/D21S11, mitochondrial DNA, Y-STR if male suspect, other physical evidence) Key teaching point: partial profiles have real value — they narrow the field, they eliminate, and they can exonerate. They just can't always provide certainty. Follow-up: connect to real cases where degraded DNA (arson, flood, decomposition) still contributed to solving the crime.
