The Scenario

Break-In at Ashford Marina

⚓  Overnight break-in at a luxury yacht moored at Ashford Marina. Thieves stole navigation electronics and a safe containing cash and documents. A blood smear was found on a shattered cabin porthole.
🧪  DNA was extracted from the blood smear, but exposure to saltwater spray and morning condensation degraded the sample. Only 9 of 14 STR loci produced reliable results — five loci are inconclusive.
🔎  Six individuals had marina key-card access that night. Each provided a DNA sample. Your job: Analyze the STR profiles to determine your prime suspect(s) and explain why others can be eliminated.
Read the scenario out loud or let students read independently. More complex than Days 1-2. Real CODIS uses 20 core STR loci (expanded from 13 in January 2017). Saltwater is a common DNA degradation factor in maritime cases — salt crystals damage the sugar-phosphate backbone. Key framing: despite losing 5 loci, 9 remaining loci is still a LOT of data. Students should be systematic — go locus by locus.
Quick Refresher

What is an STR?

🧬 Short Tandem Repeat A short DNA sequence that repeats over and over at a specific location on a chromosome
🔢 Allele Numbers The number tells you how many times the sequence repeats — e.g., 14, 17 means 14 repeats on one chromosome and 17 on the other
📍 14 CODIS Loci Forensic labs check STRs at many locations (loci) — CODIS originally used 13 core loci. More loci = more discriminating power to tell people apart
🌊 Degraded Samples Saltwater, heat, UV, or bacteria can break down DNA strands — some loci may fail to produce results, leaving gaps in the profile

A mismatch at any available locus = eliminated. But 5 degraded loci mean some suspects may remain unresolved.

Quick review — spend 30 seconds here max. Students have seen this twice now. Updates from Day 2: pill 3 now references 14 CODIS loci and discriminating power. Pill 4 mentions saltwater as a degradation factor alongside heat/UV. Key distinction: DEGRADED means no data — it's NOT a match AND is NOT a mismatch. It's simply missing.

Debrief — Plot Twist

The Lab Recovered All 5 Degraded Loci…

Suspects B & D are identical twins. Their DNA profiles are identical at every single locus — all 14 of 14.
Identical twins come from the same fertilized egg and share 100% of their nuclear DNA. No number of STR loci can distinguish them.

🔍 So What Now? STR analysis has hit its limit. Investigators need non-DNA evidence:
fingerprints, security cameras, key-card swipe logs, alibis, eyewitnesses
🧬 Cutting-Edge Science Emerging techniques can distinguish identical twins:
epigenetic profiling (DNA methylation differs) and somatic mutations (rare changes unique to each twin)
💡 Key Takeaway DNA is powerful but not omnipotent. Forensic science relies on multiple lines of evidence — not DNA alone.
The twin reveal should get a reaction. Let it land, then advance to the "So What Now?" row. Discussion prompts: — "How can two people have identical DNA at ALL 14 loci?" (Identical/monozygotic twins — same fertilized egg, 100% shared nuclear DNA.) — "If DNA can't tell them apart, is the DNA evidence useless?" (No! It still eliminated 4 of 6 suspects and narrowed the field to 2 specific people.) — "What non-DNA evidence could crack this case?" (Key-card access logs with timestamps, marina security cameras, fingerprints on the porthole, shoe impressions, phone GPS data, alibis) — "Has this actually happened in real life?" (YES — 2009 Berlin jewelry heist: identical twins both released because prosecutors couldn't prove which twin's DNA was at the scene. 2019 Marseille case: epigenetic analysis used for the first time to convict one twin.) Key teaching points: 1. DNA evidence is probabilistic and has known limitations 2. Identical twins are a fundamental limit of STR-based profiling 3. Forensic investigations always use MULTIPLE types of evidence 4. Science is advancing — epigenetic profiling and whole-genome sequencing of somatic mutations may eventually solve this problem routinely
