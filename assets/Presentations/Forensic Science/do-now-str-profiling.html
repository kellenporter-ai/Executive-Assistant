The Scenario

A Break-In at the School Lab

🔬  Last night, someone broke into the school science storage room. A small blood sample was collected from a piece of broken glass near the window.
🚨  Three suspects were identified. Investigators collected a DNA sample from each one and ran an STR analysis on all four samples — including the crime scene blood.
🧬  Your job: Compare the STR profiles to determine which suspect(s) can be eliminated — and which one matches the crime scene evidence.
Read the scenario out loud (or let students read it themselves if they're already working). Remind students: we're not proving guilt — we're using DNA evidence to eliminate or match profiles.
Quick Refresher

What is an STR?

🧬 Short Tandem Repeat A short DNA sequence that repeats over and over at a specific location on a chromosome
🔢 Allele Numbers The number tells you how many times the sequence repeats — e.g., 14, 17 means 14 repeats on one chromosome and 17 on the other
📍 Multiple Loci Forensic labs check STRs at several different locations (loci) in the genome — the combination is unique to each person

A match at all loci tested = the profiles are consistent. A mismatch at any locus = eliminated.

This is a quick review — students should have covered STRs already. Spend no more than 30 seconds on this slide. Key takeaway: even one mismatch eliminates a suspect. Multiple matches provide strong evidence of a consistent profile.

💡 Why Multiple Loci? One locus could match by chance — more loci = astronomically lower probability of a random match
Use this slide for class discussion. Reveal the table first, then the answer boxes. Discussion prompts: — "Does matching at all 4 loci PROVE Suspect A committed the crime?" (No — it only means they cannot be ruled out) — "What other evidence would investigators need?" — "Suspect B matched at vWA — does that matter? Why/why not?" Key teaching point: DNA evidence is powerful but probabilistic. It eliminates; it doesn't convict alone.
