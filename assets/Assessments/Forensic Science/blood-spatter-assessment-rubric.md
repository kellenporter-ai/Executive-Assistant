## Question 1: Blood Composition and Typing

| Skill | Missing 0% | Emerging 55% | Approaching 65% | Developing 85% | Refining 100% |
|-------|-----------|-------------|-----------------|----------------|---------------|
| I am able to describe the components of blood and explain the forensic significance of blood typing. | There is no attempt to describe blood types or their forensic significance. | There is an attempt to describe blood typing -BUT- The response confuses antigens with antibodies -OR- Does not correctly identify which antigens are present in Type B+ and Type O- blood -OR- Does not address why blood type is class evidence. | There is a decent attempt to describe blood typing and its forensic use -BECAUSE- The student correctly identifies that Type B blood has B antigens and Type O lacks A and B antigens -BUT- The explanation of Rh factor is missing or incorrect -OR- The student states blood type is class evidence but does not clearly explain why (many people share the same type) -OR- Does not contrast class evidence with individual evidence like DNA profiling. | There is a good attempt to describe blood typing and its forensic use -BECAUSE- Type B+ is correctly described as having B antigens and Rh factor, and Type O- is correctly described as lacking A, B, and Rh antigens -AND- The student explains that blood type is class evidence because many people share the same type, so it can only exclude suspects -BUT- The explanation of what individual evidence (DNA from white blood cells) could do instead is incomplete or missing. | There is an excellent attempt to describe blood typing and its forensic use -BECAUSE- Both blood types are fully and correctly described with their antigens and Rh factors -AND- The student clearly explains that the suspect's O- blood excludes them as the source of B+ blood at the scene -AND- The student explains that blood type is class evidence shared by a large percentage of the population -AND- DNA profiling from white blood cells is identified as the individual evidence needed for positive identification. |

## Question 2: Qualitative Angle of Impact

| Skill | Missing 0% | Emerging 55% | Approaching 65% | Developing 85% | Refining 100% |
|-------|-----------|-------------|-----------------|----------------|---------------|
| I am able to qualitatively assess the angle of impact of a blood drop based on whether the stain is round or elliptical, and determine the direction of travel. | There is no attempt to assess the angle of impact or direction of travel from the drop shapes. | There is an attempt to compare the two drops -BUT- The student does not correctly relate round shapes to steep angles or elliptical shapes to shallow angles -OR- The direction of travel is not identified -OR- The response simply restates that the drops look different without interpretation. | There is a decent attempt to assess angle and direction -BECAUSE- The student correctly identifies that Drop A (round) came from approximately straight down and Drop B (elliptical) came from a shallower angle -BUT- Does not explain how the pointed tail and satellites indicate direction of travel -OR- Does not use cohesion, adhesion, or surface tension to explain why the shape changes -OR- Does not provide plausible events for each drop. | There is a good attempt to assess angle and direction -BECAUSE- Both drops are correctly analyzed (round = steep/passive, elliptical = shallower angle) -AND- The student explains that the pointed tail of Drop B indicates the direction of travel and satellites break away ahead of the main drop -AND- At least one physics concept (cohesion, adhesion, or surface tension) is used correctly -BUT- The explanation of why angle changes shape could be more detailed -OR- Only one plausible event scenario is provided instead of both. | There is an excellent attempt to assess angle and direction -BECAUSE- Both drops are correctly and thoroughly analyzed -AND- Direction of travel is determined from the pointed tail and satellite positions -AND- The student explains that cohesion holds the drop together, surface tension resists spreading, and adhesion causes the blood to stick to the surface, and that at shallower angles more momentum is directed along the surface causing elongation -AND- Plausible event scenarios are provided for both drops (e.g., Drop A from passive dripping of a stationary wound, Drop B from a medium- or high-velocity impact event). |

## Question 3: Bloodstain Pattern Identification

| Skill | Missing 0% | Emerging 55% | Approaching 65% | Developing 85% | Refining 100% |
|-------|-----------|-------------|-----------------|----------------|---------------|
| I am able to identify and classify bloodstain patterns and explain what events at a crime scene created them. | There is no attempt to identify or classify the bloodstain patterns in the simulation. | There is an attempt to describe what is seen at the evidence markers -BUT- The pattern types are not correctly identified -OR- Only one marker is addressed instead of three -OR- The descriptions do not include specific observable details from the simulation. | There is a decent attempt to identify and classify the patterns -BECAUSE- The student selects three markers and provides observations of each -AND- At least one pattern is correctly identified and connected to a plausible event -BUT- One or more patterns are misidentified -OR- The explanations of what created each pattern lack specific detail. For example, the student may correctly identify passive drops but describe the cause vaguely as "blood fell." | There is a good attempt to identify and classify the patterns -BECAUSE- Three markers are selected with clear physical descriptions of what is observed -AND- At least two of the three patterns are correctly identified (e.g., passive drops, cast-off, impact spatter, swipe, transfer, or void) -AND- The events that created each pattern are explained with reasonable detail -BUT- One pattern identification or event explanation has a minor error or is underdeveloped. | There is an excellent attempt to identify and classify the patterns -BECAUSE- Three markers are selected with thorough physical descriptions citing specific details from the simulation (shape, size, arrangement, location) -AND- All three patterns are correctly identified by name -AND- The event that created each pattern is accurately and specifically explained (e.g., cast-off from the backswing of a weapon, impact spatter from a blunt force strike to a bleeding victim, void from an object blocking spatter that was later removed) -AND- Each identification is supported by observable evidence rather than guessing. |

## Question 4: Crime Scene Reconstruction

| Skill | Missing 0% | Emerging 55% | Approaching 65% | Developing 85% | Refining 100% |
|-------|-----------|-------------|-----------------|----------------|---------------|
| I am able to analyze blood evidence at a crime scene and reconstruct a sequence of events with supporting reasoning. | There is no attempt to reconstruct what happened at the crime scene. | There is an attempt to describe what happened -BUT- The narrative is not supported by specific evidence markers from the simulation -OR- The reconstruction contradicts the physical evidence -OR- Only one or two pieces of evidence are addressed. | There is a decent attempt to reconstruct events -BECAUSE- The narrative addresses at least three pieces of evidence from the simulation -AND- The general sequence of events is plausible -BUT- The reconstruction is missing key events (e.g., does not address what happened after the attack, or does not identify where the victim was) -OR- Evidence markers are not cited by number -OR- The chronological order is unclear. | There is a good attempt to reconstruct events -BECAUSE- The narrative is presented in chronological order -AND- At least four evidence markers are cited by number with correct interpretations -AND- The reconstruction addresses the victim's position, the type of attack, and post-attack events -BUT- One or two evidence markers are overlooked or their interpretation is incomplete. For example, the void pattern may not be connected to scene alteration, or the swipe may not be clearly tied to movement after the attack. | There is an excellent attempt to reconstruct events -BECAUSE- All six evidence markers are cited and correctly interpreted -AND- The narrative presents a clear chronological sequence (victim position, attack with weapon, aftermath, scene alteration) -AND- Each claim is supported by specific evidence from the simulation -AND- The reconstruction demonstrates sophisticated forensic reasoning by connecting multiple types of evidence to build a complete and coherent picture of events. |
