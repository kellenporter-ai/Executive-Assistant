Case File: The Break-In

Forensic Science -- Activity 7-2 (Obj. 7.2, 7.3)

Late last night, a break-in occurred at a local high school. Several computers were stolen from the technology lab. The building was empty at the time, but a motion detector triggered a silent alarm that alerted police.

Officers arriving at the scene found doors propped open with paper wedged in the doorjamb. Near the propped door, they discovered a cold soft-drink can that appeared to have been recently left by the intruder.

A DNA sample was obtained from the lip of the can. A convenience store clerk identified three young men who purchased canned drinks shortly before the break-in. Surveillance video confirmed the purchase, and the clerk provided names.

All three suspects were fingerprinted and provided DNA mouth swab samples. Your job: analyze the DNA evidence using gel electrophoresis to determine if any suspect's DNA matches the crime-scene DNA.

After your analysis, your class will hold a debate. You will need to argue for your conclusion.

How DNA Profiling Works

DNA profiling (also called DNA fingerprinting) compares specific regions of DNA called Short Tandem Repeats (STRs). These repeating sequences vary in length between individuals, making them useful for identification.

Gel Electrophoresis separates DNA fragments by size. An electric current pulls DNA through a gel matrix. Smaller fragments travel farther from the wells (loading point) than larger fragments.

Reading the Gel:

  • Each vertical lane contains DNA from one sample
  • The ladder (first lane) contains fragments of known sizes for reference
  • Dark bands appear where DNA fragments of a particular size have accumulated
  • Bands at the same horizontal position in different lanes indicate fragments of the same size

Matching: If a suspect's DNA profile shares all bands with the crime-scene DNA at identical positions, it is considered a match. The more STR loci tested, the stronger the identification.

Important: Some bands may appear at common positions across many people because certain alleles are widespread in the population. A single shared band does NOT mean a match.
